ExoCAM and ExoRT, Open-Source Tools for Planetary Climate Modeling and Radiative Transfer

Answering big questions about planetary climate requires powerful, flexible tools. Standard climate models are built for Earth, but what about planets with different atmospheres, orbits, or stars?

I am the primary developer and maintainer of ExoCAM, an open-source 3D climate model built upon the National Center for Atmospheric Research's (NCAR) Community Earth System Model (CESM). ExoCAM is designed to simulate the climates of a vast range of worlds, from ancient Earth to distant exoplanets orbiting other stars.

At the core of ExoCAM is ExoRT, a flexible two-stream correlated-K radiative transfer model.

ExoCAM and ExoRT are publicly available community tools that are used by numerous research groups worldwide. My focus is on developing robust, open-source software that empowers the entire field of planetary science. You can read the primary paper on ExoCAM here:

Exploring the Limits of Habitability

What makes a planet capable of supporting life? My research uses the tools I build, like ExoCAM, to map the "habitable zone" and understand what newly discovered planets might actually look like.

By simulating these alien environments in 3D, we can move beyond simple definitions of habitability. I've explored the climates of key targets like the **TRAPPIST-1** system, the resilience of habitable climates on planets in **circumbinary star systems** (like Tatooine), and the fundamental physics that sets the **"inner edge"** of the habitable zone—the point where a planet's oceans would boil away.

Our Planet's Alien Past (and Future)

Long before humans, Earth itself was an "alien" world. For billions of years, life existed under a Sun that was up to 25% fainter than it is today. How did life survive, and what did our planet look like?

My foundational research investigated this **"Faint Young Sun Paradox."** I used climate models to show that **fractal organic hazes**, similar to those on Saturn's moon Titan, could have formed in the Archean atmosphere. These hazes would have provided a critical UV shield for early life while still allowing the planet to stay warm—transforming the early Earth into a "Pale Orange Dot."

What's Next? Current & Future Research

Science is always moving. My current work is focused on bridging the gap between our theoretical models and the first-ever observations of terrestrial exoplanets from telescopes like the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST).

I am leading a multi-model collaboration to interpret potential observations of the well-known **TRAPPIST-1e** system. By comparing the results from many different climate and chemistry models, we can better understand what we are *really* seeing and provide a more robust interpretation of a planet's atmosphere.

My future research will dive deeper into one of the most complex and important topics in planetary climate: **atmospheric hazes**. With new funding from NASA, my next major project will explore how hazes form on planets orbiting different types of stars. This work will help us understand how hazes affect a planet's climate and, critically, how they would influence its observable spectrum.
